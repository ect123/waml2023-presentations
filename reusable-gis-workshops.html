In the Olden Days

Pre 2019, teaching material

  • Many internal shared folders
  • Non-portable and non-reproducible
  • Uncoordinated approaches
In the olden days, long ago in the year 2019 and before, we didn't really have a solid method for creating, storing, and sharing our learning material. Things tended to be shared on network drives in folders, and often hard to find. They tended to be things like Word docs or PPT presentation that may not have had any version control or file histories attached, so in ways they were non-portable and non-reproducible. And our team approaches to creating and storing them were not well coordinated, leaving room for duplication of work and lessening the possibility for collaboration.

Other challenges

  • Info literacy + GIS teaching
  • Keeping up with current trends in research and practice
  • Accommodating teaching in busy calendars
And mixed in with inefficient workflows for creating and storing our Learning material, there were other challenges we were facing as well. Any of you who have responsibilities around teaching GIS, other areas of digital scholarship, you know that it can be very difficult to be a data management and informaiton literacy expert as well as a GIS instructor. These are oftten additional responsibilities that fall on our shoulders. We're expected to be the technical experts, to know the current trends, the have time to update and adapt teaching content in response to new software versions, and fit scheduling workshop events into our already busy calendars.

Original Goals

  • Provide teaching opportunites
  • Pedagogical upskilling
  • Develop new approcahes to developing teaching material
We recognized we can't always provide a wide range of up-to-date and relavent technical workshops to our community. At the same time, we knew that there are graduate students who have advanced skillsets in these areas, many of whom are hoping to gain instructional experience as they prepare to enter their professions. So we wanted to be able to somehow provide oportunities to student exerts – who have very relavent skillsets and are often more knowledgeable on current trends in teaching these skills that we realistically have the capacity for. We wanted to be able to upskill ourselves and students and experiment with teaching workflows. And here were especially inspired by the Carpentries OER model. And of course we wanted to develop these workflows in a way that alleviated some of the issues we were experiencing with our previous ones, being more reproducebible and collaborative.

Developing Reusable Technology Workshops to Enhance Digital Literacy

So in November 2019 we ended up applying for a grant from UBC's Teaching and Learning Enhancement Fund. Or TLEF. This fund is meant "to enrich student learning by supporting innovative and effective educational enhancements", The title of our project was Developing Reusable Technology Workshops to Enhance Digital Literacy. In early 2020 with TLEF funding we hired 4 graduate teaching assistants (or GTA's) to develop and deliver a series of 6 modular workshops on GIS / GIScience.

Current approaches

UBC Library GIS workshop development and delivery workflows originate from TLEF grant

That was 2020. We renewed the project with TLEF in 2021 for creating digital scholarship workshops - very similar process with a couple of refinements. Today, We still use a lot of the original workflows that came from the TLEF grant to develop and deliver GIS workshops, but I think we've adapted to change with other realities like, the number of weekly hours students have available and the upfront time it takes to create a new workshop from scratch.

Still using GitHub

We haven't found a better replacement for using GitHub to manage the development of workshop content. The tools built into GitHub offer so much for collaboration, monitoring work, assigning work, checking edits and updates, and having conversations about specific elements of the content that's being worked on.

Our workflow for developing workshops

  1. Topic decided
  2. Website from template
  3. Iterative refinement
  4. Project archiving
Our general approach is to first decide on a topic for the workshop - in most cases these have been related to one of the existing workshops (either developed through the TLEF grant, or by me). Then a new workshop website is create on GitHub with a draft of the workshop content. the next part is iteratively improving the site over a period of time before delivery. Typically, students and I use GitHub Issues to dissect the workshop-in-progress into small to-dos. GitHub Issues make it really efficient to document progress and assign tasks. So when we meet (usually weekly or bi-weekly) we can review the workshop, create issues for work that still needs to be done, and close certain issues that have been completed. We also create issues live when delivering workshops as a way of making notes for things that come up live - like spelling errors or requests from attendees for clarification, etc. Finally – and this part is still new to us - is to archive the project in Github. We have a lot of content in GitHub that was created by students that have graduated, and maybe we no longer want to offer those workshops in their current form. In we do have processes to add a note to the public-facing site saying that a particular workshop is no longer being updated or maintained, and we can also make this apparent on the GitHub backend where we archive the repository and lock it from further development. That doesn't mean we can't dulicate it at a later time and resurrect the workshop.

Reusing

  1. Designed to be "modular"
  2. Open license
  3. Integrated with GitHub
And finally, we really value the fact that these are open and allowed to be reused or remixed. We've always tried to design workshops in a way that makes them "modular" so that someone can take a lot of the content and reuse it outside of a UBC Library-delivered workshop. We VERY often refer people to our workshops in consultations - if they have questions about Leaflet, or map projections, or Storymaps - we have some ready-made content for them to refer to. I also know that our map projections workshop is regualrly used as a module in an Intro to GIS course in Geography. We also apply an open license CC Attribution 4.0 International, and make that very apparent in our template. And finally, because we're tied into the GitHub ecosystem, there are lots of ways anyone from outside of our organization can find and copy the workshop content to use for their own purposes.
