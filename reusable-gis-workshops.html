The challenges

Any of you who have responsibilities around teaching GIS, digital scholarship, or other technical skills, you know that it can be very challenging. As library and information professionals, we often assume the position of data management and informaiton literacy expert among our academic communities. And we often teach those topics in the classroom. With functional and technical roles, like GIS, many of us are additionally expected to be the technical experts, to know the current trends, the have time to update and adapt teaching content in response to new software versions, and fit scheduling into our already busy calendars. These are some of the challenges the UBC Library Research commons has attempted to alleviate through finding ways to work collaboratively with UBC's student experts.

Original idea

Our original idea stemmed from recognizing we can't always provide a wide range of up-to-date and relavent technical workshops to our community. At the same time, we recgonize that there are graduate students who have advanced skillsets in these areas, many of whom are hoping to gain instructional experience as they prepare to enter an academic profession. UBC Teaching and Learning Enhancement Fund (TLEF) Grant

to enrich student learning by supporting innovative and effective educational enhancements
Luckily, at UBC there is a funding source that's meant "to enrich student learning by supporting innovative and effective educational enhancements", and we decided to apply in November 2019. In 2020 and 2021 we hired eight graduate teaching assistants to develop and deliver a series of 12 modular workshops on GIScience and Digital Scholarship topics. In year one we focused on GISci and in year two we focused in Digital scholarship. The project also established a workflow for publishing open, reusable instructional content using GitHub. One goal of this project was for us to figure out ways to better streamline the creation, development, delivery, and iterative improvemnets of our workshop offerings, and we felt that GitHub was a great solution. We were already using it for GIS workshops somewhat, so there was some precidence for using it. But mainly it offered us tools to collaborate, share, update, and manage our work all in one platform. It also gave us ways to easily reproduce workshops, publish them online, and apply open licenses. The workflows for creating workshops we developed as part of these grants is now standard for UBC Library Research Commons workshops.
